Hiragana vs katakana: two alphabets for the exact same ~46 sounds — think lowercase vs UPPERCASE. Hiragana (the curvy set) writes native Japanese words and all the grammar glue. Katakana (the angular set) writes foreign loanwords — which is most golf, food, and hotel vocabulary, plus your name.

The sound shifts — hundreds are ひゃく hyaku and thousands are せん sen, except these five (it's your dakuten pattern again — h→b, h→p):

Japan counts in 10,000s. The unit is まん man: 10,000 = いちまん (one man), 120,000 = じゅうにまん (twelve man). A ¥198,000 driver is じゅうきゅうまん はっせん えん — nineteen man, eight thousand. Prices end in えん en, and you ask with いくらですか。
Ordering things uses its own 1-5: ひとつ ふたつ みっつ よっつ いつつ (hitotsu futatsu mittsu yottsu itsutsu) — ビール ふたつ ください. People: ひとり (1), ふたり (2), さんにん (3).

The drill is production-first, both directions. See ¥ → say it: say the price out loud, reveal, self-grade. Hear it → type ¥: listen, type the digits. Real register speed is the goal.
