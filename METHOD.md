# Method — how these lessons work (and why not Duolingo)

## Why Duolingo fails for this goal

Duolingo optimizes for streaks, not speech. Its core loop is **recognition** —
tap the right word bubble, pick from multiple choice. Recognition and production
are different memory pathways: you can "know" a phrase on Duolingo for months and
still freeze when a caddie asks you something. It also drips useless sentences
("the owl eats bread") instead of the 500 phrases a golf tourist actually says.
None of that here.

## The technique stack (what research + polyglot practice agree on)

### 1. Production-first drilling (Pimsleur-style anticipation)

Every drill is: **prompt in Korean → you produce the Japanese out loud → then see the answer.**
Never multiple choice, never "arrange these bubbles". Struggling to retrieve for
3 seconds is the mechanism — that struggle is what writes the memory (retrieval
practice / testing effect, the single best-replicated result in learning science).

### 2. Spaced repetition (SRS), but on whole phrases

Anki-style expanding intervals: miss it → see it next session; get it → 3 days →
1 week → 2 weeks. We run this manually through the review queue in `PROGRESS.md`.
Unit of memorization is the **phrase/sentence, not the word** (sentence mining à la
Refold/AJATT) — you'll never need "beer" alone, you need "biiru futatsu kudasai".

### 3. Pattern + swap, not grammar rules (Michel Thomas style)

Learn one pattern ("~ o kudasai"), then generate 20 sentences by swapping the slot.
You build sentences from minute one instead of studying conjugation tables.
Your Korean gives this superpowers — the patterns are structurally identical.

### 4. Shadowing (Arguelles)

For pronunciation and fluency: listen to a native line, speak over/after it at
full speed, copying rhythm and flatness. Every lesson marks 3-5 lines as
shadowing lines. Speaking-only goal = shadowing matters more than anything visual.

### 5. Scenario immersion (task-based learning + comprehensible input)

Each unit ends with a full roleplay: Claude plays the konbini clerk / caddie /
restaurant staff **in Japanese**, slightly above your level (i+1), and you get
through the scenario. Failing and repairing in the roleplay is the point —
that's Unit 8's phrases getting free practice.

### 6. Frequency-first vocabulary (the 80/20 rule)

~1,000 well-chosen words cover ~85% of daily conversation. We only learn words
that appear in YOUR scenarios (golf, food, shopping, transport). No animals,
no colors-for-the-sake-of-colors, no "the boy reads a newspaper".

### 7. Speak from day 1 (Benny Lewis) — imperfectly, out loud

Every session ends with you producing full sentences aloud, mistakes included.
Perfectionism is the enemy; a wrong particle with confident delivery beats
silence. Japanese service staff complete your sentences anyway.

## Session shape (every drill session)

1. **2 min** — review queue from `PROGRESS.md` (SRS: yesterday's misses first)
2. **10 min** — new pattern + swap drill (production-first, out loud)
3. **5 min** — shadowing lines
4. **5 min** — scenario roleplay using old + new material
5. **1 min** — log mistakes to lesson file, update `PROGRESS.md`, commit

Short and frequent beats long and rare: 20 minutes daily > 2 hours weekly
(spacing effect). If a session must be tiny, do only step 1.
